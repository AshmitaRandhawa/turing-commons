Bias Cards A Tool for Responsible Research and Innovation

Bias Cards

The following cards provide simple descriptions for the respective bias that they represent. They are categorised according to whether they represent social, statistical or cognitive biases. This tool is intended to be used alongside the responsible research and innovation workshop activites. A more complete guide for biases in data science and AI can be found here.

Social Biases click any of the cards to reveal information

Historical Bias

Structural biases arise within the context of a data science or AI project when there is a gap or misalignment between the state of the world and the goals or objectives that are encoded and propagated in the model or system being developed. For instance, even with perfect sampling and feature selection, a model that perpetuates (or exacerbates) socioeconomic inequalities through the outcomes it promotes is contributing to historical or structural bias.

Historical Bias

Historical (or structural) biases exist prior to the inception of any data science project.

Label Bias

This is a particular risk where a proxy has been used that occludes inequalities between groups of subjects, as it can create disparities in the validity of the model outcomes.
toggle card

Label Bias

A label (or feature) within your dataset may not mean the same thing for all data subjects.

Missing Data

Missing data can make accurate predictions or classifications challenging to produce. Although this is also a statistical concern, the social cause of the missing data may be the result of non-random but statistically informative events (e.g.m socioeconomic barriers that make it difficult for certain groups to participate).

Missing Data

Data may be missing for a variety of reasons and it can be difficult to uncover the reasons.

Statistical Biases click any of the cards to reveal information

Chronological Bias

This is an issue when the chronological difference results in individuals being subjected to different exposures or interventions based on the time their data were recorded. For instance, in observational studies related to the effects of COVID-19, individual patients or cohorts received different treatment due to changing healthcare practices.
toggle card

Chronological Bias

Arises when individuals in the dataset are added at different times.
toggle card

Training-Serving Skew

This skew can be caused by:

  • A discrepancy between how you handle data in the training and serving pipelines.
  • A change in the data between when you train and when you serve.
  • A feedback loop between your model and your algorithm.

toggle card

Training-Serving Skew

A difference between performance during training and performance during serving

Aggregation Bias

Can arise when distinct sub-groups (of a heterogeneous population) are inappropriately combined, or differences between them ignored, resulting in a loss of information and the development of a model that is more effective for one sub-group over another.
toggle card

Aggregation Bias

Confusing the units of analysis so that one concludes that what is true for the group must be true for the sub-group or individual.

Cognitive Biases click any of the cards to reveal information

Confirmation Bias

This bias can cause people to dismiss or downplay the significance of information that disconfirms their favoured hypothesis. This can be the result of motivated reasoning or sub-conscious attitudes, which in turn may lead to prejudicial judgements that are not based on reasoned evidence.
toggle card

Confirmation Bias

A tendency to search for, gather, or use information that confirms pre-existing ideas and beliefs.
toggle card

Biases of Rhetoric

Such biases systematically mislead readers into viewing the results of a study in a more favourable (or unfavourable) light. These biases may be more prevalent in “popular” or “fashionable” topics, where there may be additional incentives to oversell the impact of the study
toggle card

Biases of Rhetoric

A group of related biases that refer to the use of persuasive techniques, opinions, or modes of argumentation that steer the reader to adopt a particular belief, attitude, or policy, which is not sufficiently justifiable on the basis of the evidence.
toggle card

Availability Heuristic

This bias can occur as a result of some information being more readily retrievable or recalled more quickly than other (less available) information. For example, someone may believe that an explanation for a social or political event is more likely than others due to its prevalence in the media.
toggle card

Availability Heuristic

People tend to overestimate the importance of information that is available to them.
